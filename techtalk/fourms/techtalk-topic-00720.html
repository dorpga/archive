TI-99/4a vs Commodore vs PC SMACKDOWN!!!

by maxell » Thu Aug 07, 2014 8:39 pm

Welcome to the EVENT OF THE [insert arbitrary unit of time here]!







Starting with the TI-99/4a - Why it's bad

The TI-99/4a was a 16-bit personal computer released in 1981.
The graphics of the system are a measly 16 colours, unlike the commodore's IFLI mode which allows 128 colours.
The games release on the TI are simple, like Space Invaders or Hunt The Wumpus. On the commodore and later PC, games got much better.

The chip used in the TI, the TMS9900, is very unknown and it's hard to find assembly resources for this chip, as opposed to the common 6502 and x86 chips used in the Commodore and PC.

The music chip had 3 voices but it was nothing special.


Title screen of Hunt the Wumpus for the TI-99/4a.
The graphics are very simplistic and the gameplay itself is not that deep.

The Commodore 64 - Why it's good

The commodore 64 was commodore's shinier and better VIC 20, a machine they had previously. The VIC-20 itself was not a great machine, but the 64 was.
The graphics of the system were amazing for 1982. Atari and it's 2600 game console were popular at this time and the Commodore's graphics were much better.
C64 software was amazing, and games like The Great Giana Sisters and Maniac Mansion showed that this machine was even better than the newest, shiniest, just-came-out game console known as the NES.
SID is the name of Commodore's music chip which produced techno music people want to dance to! People started putting SID music everywhere on the C64 and it became famous as an amazing music chip that some say even beat 1989's hot Sega Genesis game console.


Screenshot of International Karate Plus for the C64.
The game has many beautiful levels and support for 3 players at once.

The IBM PC - Why you're probably using it right now
The IBM PC was released in 1981 by IBM. If we're talking about that original machine, even the TI would win over that.
It could only do text mode with the MDA card originally, and sound was limited to one channel bleeps.
It's crap.

But...on came the clones. Eventually the IBM PC as a platform became the most powerful. It just kept growing. In the 1980s, this meant games like Thexder.


Screenshot of Thexder for the IBM PC.
It's using 4-colour graphics from the CGA card. Not pretty.

In the 1990s, IBM as a business drifted away from the production of the machines. Pretty soon OEMs like Compaq and Packard Bell were moving units. The most used PC operating system shifted from Microsoft BASIC (used on the Commodore 64 as well) to Microsoft's MS-DOS and later Windows OSes.

Eventually, VGA graphics and 256-colours were the norm. Everybody and their dog had an IBM PC compatible.
Sound went from bleeps and blips to the Sound Blaster, a sound card that could produce amazing digitized and wavetable sound.


Id software's DOOM was released in 1993.
It used 256 colour VGA graphics, and it was a famous first person shooter.

In 1995 or so, the SVGA graphics mode became popular. Microsoft released Windows '95, and a whole bunch of games to go along with it.


Windows '95 doing stuff.

In the 2000s, games became a LOT better. Bungie released Halo, Microsoft released Windows XP, and a new operating system was gaining popularity.

The DirectX and OpenGL frameworks allowed IBM PC (now just PC) graphics cards to create amazing experiences.


Halo, a first person shooter created in 2001.
It runs on Microsoft Windows XP.

But a new OS (well, kernel) was gaining popularity. People started distributing it and many thought that Windows had a monopoly, and the perfect way to stop it was to use not Mac, but...

LINUX! This operating system created a big fat land bridge between Unix on a mainframe and the uninhabited desktop Unix island.

Distributions like Ubuntu ("Linux for human beings") prided themselves as being a free and faster alternative to Microsoft Windows.
Games grew bigger, and the first person shooter genre EXPLODED. PCs were now seen as more fit to play music than a CD player.

The Internet became more used than ever.


Youtube is still a great site to share videos.
This is what it looked like in the 2000s.


Linux distributions like Ubuntu still haven't quite beaten MS Windows.
