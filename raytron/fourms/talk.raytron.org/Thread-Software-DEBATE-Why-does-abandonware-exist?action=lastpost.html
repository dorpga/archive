This was once a topic in the IRC but considering this forum isn't as active and there's also some people who aren't on the IRC who hold some rather unpopular opinions.

I'm going to define abandonware as software that is over 10 years old and has little usage in North America. So basically stuff like Windows '98, Office '97, System 7, anything like that.

My argument:

The reason for abandonware's existence, and any of the clichÃ videos like that on youtube (TV logo history compilations, VHS logo compilations, Windows versions real and fake, etc.) is because of...mundane commercialization. Specifically, studying and documenting the history of it. It's about taking things we don't notice, and then documenting all of it.

Why is that needed? Well, I bet you've noticed most people doing this are children. And what, per se, did children of the 2000s see a lot? Well, Windows XP for sure. I bet they also watched TV - and saw TV identification bumpers at the end of each show. And many people took them for granted. But, some kids didn't. And so the study of the history of software was born from curiosity about ubiquitous and mundane things.

So, simple curiosity might be the reason people started studying everything. But why does it seem so fascinating to these people, and why do they keep rambling on about it? Well, the same reason anything that was different in the past seems fascinating to some people: it shows the evolution of the world as we know it. In the case of computers, the tools we use every day. Learning that such a ubiquitous tool was so different, so recently, is mind-blowing to an 8 year old. So, they dig deeper. It no longer becomes when and what was created, but how it was created. This is beta collecting.

And of course, there's an easy explanation for all the fake versions and nonsense startup screens - kids being silly. They screw around with it.

TL;DR: Abandonware exists because of curiosity about mundane tools, which then grows into a fascination about the history of software.

IMO, I think abandonware exists because it is like VHS tapes, with VHS tapes being rarely used, if at all. There may be that one person who purely relies on VHS and not DVDs. Abandonware is similar in that there may be one person who doesn't have enough money to buy a new computer, and that person enjoys games that won't run on XP or later. It would make sense for that person to share it so that other people can enjoy what he likes and uses, and this may convince other people to want to play more.
