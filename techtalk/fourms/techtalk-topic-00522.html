william.1008 wrote:USB ports are fine for a laptop because it makes it a lot more portable, but on a desktop, no way. There enough space to have at less one of pretty much every port, why not use it? I also think desktop computers should have floppy drives so old files can be easily converted with the need for the external drive. You can add a floppy diskette drive to any modern PC you get, it just doesn't come standard anymore because not many people use them on a regular basis and it saves money to not include one from the factory.

Also, although you (and many other PC people) hate all-in-one systems, these are not aimed at people like you and me. These systems are geared more for the kinds of people that don't use their PCs for much more other than to check their emails and go on Facebook. To those people, these all-in-one systems are great! They don't take up much desk space and they don't have to worry about managing a bunch of cables and stuff around. If you don't do much with your PC that is the kind of system that is geared for you.

Now, the reason many manufacturers are moving out of having many different ports on the back of their systems to adding a bunch of USB ports is because USB is universal. The idea is, that if you have any piece of USB hardware, you can plug it into any system and it will work almost immediately without having to install a bunch of specialized drivers. USB openes a whole new world in the PC industry for devices and such. Before this, many different things that you plugged in would use many different types of ports including serial ports, parallel ports, game controller ports, etc. For some people, this was hard to deal with because some PCs didn't have the necessary ports to use the hardware they needed.

Another problem from before, was that when you plugged anything into one of these serial ports and such, they were sometimes a big pain to get working because there was no simple "plug-and-play" feature and you had to manually install these drivers. With USB a simple standard was set that allowed users to plug any device in and it would start working almost immediately with little to no setup required. With this, manufacturers began fazing out these other port types because anything that used them could be replaced with a USB equivalent. Not to mention all the adapters for mice and keyboards and such to use with USB.If you can name any piece of hardware that you would use on a computer before that wasn't a USB device, there is most likely a USB equivalent somewhere around that you could use. Just think, now there are USB floppy drives, USB DVD/CD-ROM drives, etc. USB is dominant to other port types in many ways and if you look at my old systems, I even have USB hubs on them to increase the amount of ports because I'm using all two of the available ports on the systems!
Just because something is old doesn't mean it's useless, you just need to find more creative ways to use it!
